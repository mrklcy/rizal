The Man Behind the Legend

Jos√© Protasio Rizal Mercado y Alonso Realonda was born on June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna. He became the Philippines' foremost national hero not through violent revolution, but through his brilliant mind, compassionate heart, and unwavering dedication to his country's freedom.

A polymath of extraordinary talent, Rizal was a physician, novelist, poet, sculptor, painter, educator, and linguist. His novels "Noli Me Tangere" and "El Filibusterismo" exposed the injustices of Spanish colonial rule and awakened the Filipino consciousness.

Though he advocated for peaceful reform rather than violent revolution, his writings and ideals inspired the Philippine Revolution and continue to guide the Filipino people today.

Republic Act 1425: The Rizal Law

Overview & Purpose

‚ñº

Republic Act 1425, commonly known as the Rizal Law, was enacted on June 12, 1956. Authored by Senator Claro M. Recto, this legislation mandates all educational institutions in the Philippines to include the study of Jos√© Rizal's life, works, and writings in their curricula.

The primary purpose of the Rizal Law is to inspire patriotism and nationalism among Filipino students by exposing them to Rizal's ideals and contributions to Philippine independence. It aims to ensure that future generations continue to appreciate and learn from Rizal's legacy.

Key Provisions

‚ñº

The Rizal Law contains several important provisions:

  • Section 1: Requires all schools, colleges, and universities to include in their curricula courses on the life, works, and writings of Jos√© Rizal, particularly his novels "Noli Me Tangere" and "El Filibusterismo."
  • Section 2: Mandates that all public and private libraries must have adequate copies of these works in their original (Spanish) or translated versions.
  • Section 3: Authorizes the printing and distribution of Rizal's works by the Board of National Education.
  • Section 4: Appropriates funds for the implementation of the law.

Historical Context & Controversy

‚ñº

The passage of the Rizal Law was not without controversy. The Catholic Church strongly opposed it, arguing that Rizal's novels contained anti-Catholic sentiments. This opposition led to a compromise where students could be exempted from reading the novels if they or their parents objected on religious grounds.

Despite this controversy, the law was successfully passed, reflecting the government's commitment to promoting nationalism and honoring Rizal's contributions to Philippine independence.

Educational Impact

‚ñº

The Rizal Law has had a profound impact on Philippine education:

  • It established "Rizal courses" as mandatory in all levels of education
  • It ensured that Rizal's novels, which critique social injustices and advocate for reform, remain accessible to students
  • It helped preserve Rizal's legacy and ideas for future generations
  • It contributed to the development of Filipino national identity and consciousness

Today, courses on Rizal's life and works remain a standard part of Philippine higher education, ensuring that his ideals continue to inspire Filipino students.

19th Century Philippines as Rizal's Context

Spanish Colonial Administration

The Philippines in the 19th century was under Spanish colonial rule, which had been established since the 16th century. The colonial administration was headed by a Governor-General who represented the Spanish Crown and held both civil and military powers.

The country was divided into provinces (alcald√≠as) governed by alcaldes mayores, and further subdivided into towns (pueblos) administered by gobernadorcillos, who were usually members of the local elite.

This system was characterized by:

  • Centralized authority in Manila
  • Limited participation of native Filipinos in governance
  • Corruption and abuse of power by Spanish officials
  • Use of forced labor (polo y servicios)
  • Collection of excessive taxes and tributes

Social Hierarchy in Colonial Philippines

The social structure in 19th century Philippines was highly stratified, with clear divisions based on race, wealth, and political power:

  • Peninsulares: Spanish-born Spaniards who occupied the highest government positions
  • Insulares/Creoles: Spaniards born in the Philippines who held some government positions but faced discrimination from peninsulares
  • Principal√≠a: The native elite class that included wealthy landowners and government officials
  • Ilustrados: The educated class that emerged in the late 19th century, many educated in Europe
  • Mestizos: People of mixed Spanish-Filipino or Chinese-Filipino ancestry who often engaged in trade and commerce
  • Indios: The native Filipino majority who worked as farmers, laborers, and servants

Rizal belonged to the principal√≠a class and later became a prominent ilustrado, which gave him unique insights into both Filipino society and European ideas.

The Catholic Church and Friar Power

The Catholic Church wielded enormous influence in 19th century Philippines, with Spanish friars controlling many aspects of Filipino life:

  • Friars from various religious orders (Augustinians, Franciscans, Dominicans, Recollects) administered parishes throughout the country
  • They controlled education, with the University of Santo Tomas being the oldest university in Asia
  • Friars owned vast tracts of agricultural land (friar lands), making them powerful landlords
  • They acted as intermediaries between the colonial government and the native population
  • They censored books, newspapers, and other publications

The abuses of the friars became a central theme in Rizal's novels, particularly in "Noli Me Tangere," where he criticized their moral corruption, exploitation of the natives, and opposition to progress and education.

Education in 19th Century Philippines

The education system in 19th century Philippines was primarily controlled by the Catholic Church and had several key characteristics:

  • Primary education was limited and focused on religious instruction
  • Higher education was available mainly to the elite and wealthy families
  • The University of Santo Tomas was the primary institution for higher learning
  • The curriculum emphasized theology, philosophy, and classical studies
  • Scientific and technical education was severely lacking
  • Teaching was conducted primarily in Spanish, which most Filipinos did not speak

Rizal himself benefited from education at Ateneo Municipal and University of Santo Tomas before pursuing further studies in Europe. His experiences with both Philippine and European education systems informed his advocacy for educational reforms in the Philippines.

Economic Conditions and Trade

The economy of 19th century Philippines underwent significant changes, particularly after the opening of the country to international trade in 1834:

  • Agriculture was the backbone of the economy, with cash crops like sugar, abaca (Manila hemp), tobacco, and coconut being major exports
  • The galleon trade between Manila and Acapulco ended in 1815, leading to direct trade with Europe and America
  • Foreign merchants, particularly British and American, established businesses in Manila
  • Chinese merchants dominated local retail trade
  • The hacienda system concentrated land ownership among the elite
  • Most Filipinos remained subsistence farmers or tenant farmers (kasama)

These economic conditions created stark inequalities that Rizal observed and criticized in his writings. His own family experienced economic hardship when they were evicted from their lands in Calamba due to disputes with the Dominican friars who owned the estate.

Early Childhood (1861-1871)

‚ñº

Jos√© Rizal's early childhood was marked by several formative experiences:

  • Birth and Baptism: Born on June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna, and baptized as Jos√© Protasio Rizal Mercado y Alonso Realonda
  • Family Environment: Grew up in a wealthy family that valued education and culture
  • Early Signs of Intelligence: Showed remarkable intelligence at a young age, learning to read and write by age 5
  • Mother's Influence: Received his first lessons from his mother, who taught him reading, writing, and Catholic prayers
  • Artistic Talent: Displayed early artistic talent, creating clay and wax sculptures
  • Love for Literature: Enjoyed hearing stories and poems from his mother and elder relatives
  • Religious Upbringing: Raised in a devout Catholic household, with daily prayers and religious practices

A significant event in his childhood was witnessing the unjust arrest of his mother on false charges of attempting to poison her sister-in-law. Though she was eventually released, this experience gave young Rizal his first exposure to the injustices of Spanish colonial authorities.

Early Education (1871-1877)

‚ñº

Rizal's formal education began at a young age and was characterized by excellence:

  • First Tutor: Initially tutored at home by Maestro Celestino and Maestro Lucas Padua
  • Bi√±an School: At age 8, studied in Bi√±an under Maestro Justiniano Aquino Cruz, where he excelled in Latin and Spanish
  • Return to Calamba: After two years in Bi√±an, continued his education under private tutors in Calamba
  • Ateneo Municipal: In 1872, at age 11, enrolled at Ateneo Municipal de Manila, a prestigious Jesuit school
  • Academic Excellence: Consistently earned top grades at Ateneo, graduating with highest honors (sobresaliente) in 1877
  • Literary Development: Wrote poetry and participated in literary competitions at Ateneo
  • Scientific Interest: Developed interest in natural sciences, particularly under the guidance of Jesuit priest Francisco de Paula S√°nchez

At Ateneo, Rizal was exposed to a liberal education that emphasized science, humanities, and physical education. This Jesuit education would significantly shape his intellectual development and worldview.

Formative Influences

‚ñº

Several key influences shaped Rizal's early development:

  • Family Values: His family instilled in him values of education, hard work, and dignity
  • Brother Paciano: His elder brother Paciano, who had connections with liberal thinkers, introduced him to ideas about freedom and justice
  • The Story of the Moth: As a child, Rizal was fascinated by the story of a moth that flew into a flame, which his mother interpreted as a symbol of sacrifice
  • Jesuit Education: The Jesuits at Ateneo encouraged critical thinking and scientific inquiry
  • Filipino Identity: Despite his privileged background, Rizal experienced discrimination as a native Filipino, which fostered his sense of national identity
  • Literary Works: Early exposure to European literature, including works by Cervantes and Alexandre Dumas
  • Martyrdom of GOMBURZA: The execution of three Filipino priests (Gomez, Burgos, and Zamora) in 1872 for alleged involvement in a mutiny deeply affected Rizal and contributed to his nationalist awakening

These early influences laid the foundation for Rizal's later development as a nationalist, reformist, and intellectual leader.

Rizal's Life: Higher Education and Life Abroad

1877-1882

University of Santo Tomas

After graduating from Ateneo, Rizal enrolled at the University of Santo Tomas to study medicine. He also took courses in Philosophy and Letters. However, he experienced discrimination from Dominican professors and felt that the education was outdated compared to European universities.

1882-1885

Universidad Central de Madrid

Rizal left the Philippines in 1882 to continue his medical studies in Spain. At Universidad Central de Madrid, he completed his licentiate in medicine and also earned a degree in Philosophy and Letters. In Madrid, he became active in the Propaganda Movement, advocating for reforms in the Philippines.

1885-1887

Further Studies in Europe

Rizal pursued specialized studies in ophthalmology in Paris under Dr. Louis de Wecker and later in Heidelberg, Germany under Dr. Otto Becker. During this time, he also immersed himself in European culture, learning multiple languages and studying art, science, and politics.

1887

Publication of Noli Me Tangere

While in Berlin, Rizal completed and published his first novel, "Noli Me Tangere" (Touch Me Not). The novel exposed the abuses of Spanish colonial rule in the Philippines, particularly by the friars. It was immediately controversial and was banned in the Philippines.

Academic Achievements

‚ñº

Rizal's academic achievements were remarkable and diverse:

  • Medical Expertise: Completed his medical education and specialized in ophthalmology, becoming a skilled eye surgeon
  • Multilingualism: Mastered at least 22 languages, including Spanish, French, German, English, Italian, Japanese, Arabic, Greek, and Latin
  • Scientific Research: Conducted research in various scientific fields, including zoology, botany, ethnography, and anthropology
  • Literary Accomplishments: Wrote novels, essays, poetry, and scientific papers that were recognized for their quality
  • Artistic Skills: Developed talents in painting, sculpture, and drawing
  • Historical Research: Conducted historical research on pre-colonial Philippines, annotating Antonio de Morga's "Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas"

His education in Europe exposed him to liberal ideas and scientific advances that were not available in the Philippines, broadening his perspective and informing his vision for his country's future.

Life in Europe

‚ñº

Rizal's years in Europe were formative for his intellectual and political development:

  • Madrid (1882-1885): Studied medicine and philosophy, joined the Circulo Hispano-Filipino, and began writing for La Solidaridad newspaper
  • Paris (1885-1886): Specialized in ophthalmology and immersed himself in French culture and politics
  • Heidelberg (1886): Continued his medical studies and learned German
  • Berlin (1886-1887): Completed "Noli Me Tangere" and engaged with German intellectuals
  • London (1888-1889): Researched at the British Museum and annotated Morga's historical work
  • Brussels (1890-1891): Wrote "El Filibusterismo" and continued his advocacy work

During his time in Europe, Rizal was exposed to democratic ideals, scientific advances, and liberal political thought that greatly influenced his vision for Philippine reform.

The Propaganda Movement

‚ñº

While in Europe, Rizal became a key figure in the Propaganda Movement, a peaceful campaign for political reforms in the Philippines:

  • La Solidaridad: Contributed articles to this newspaper published by Filipino expatriates in Spain
  • Reform Goals: Advocated for equal rights for Filipinos, representation in the Spanish Cortes, secularization of parishes, and educational reforms
  • Peaceful Approach: Believed in achieving reforms through peaceful means rather than violent revolution
  • Collaboration: Worked with other ilustrados like Marcelo H. del Pilar, Graciano L√≥pez Jaena, and Mariano Ponce
  • Literary Weapons: Used his novels and essays to expose colonial abuses and advocate for change

The Propaganda Movement, though ultimately unsuccessful in achieving its immediate goals, helped raise awareness about Philippine issues and laid the groundwork for the later revolutionary movement.

Return to the Philippines and Final Years

‚ñº

Rizal's return to the Philippines and his final years were marked by continued advocacy and ultimate sacrifice:

  • First Return (1887-1888): Returned briefly after publishing "Noli Me Tangere," but faced persecution and was forced to leave again
  • Second Return (1892): Returned to establish La Liga Filipina, a peaceful organization for reform
  • Exile in Dapitan (1892-1896): Exiled to Dapitan, Zamboanga del Norte, where he practiced medicine, taught, and conducted scientific research
  • Volunteer Service: Volunteered as a military doctor in Cuba during the Spanish-American War
  • Arrest and Trial: Arrested on charges of rebellion and sedition upon his return from Cuba
  • Execution (December 30, 1896): Executed by firing squad at Bagumbayan (now Luneta Park) in Manila

His execution sparked outrage and helped galvanize the Philippine Revolution, making him a martyr for the cause of Philippine independence.

Rizal's Revolutionary Novels

Jos√© Rizal wrote two groundbreaking novels that exposed the injustices of Spanish colonial rule and awakened Filipino nationalism. These works became catalysts for the Philippine Revolution.

Noli Me Tangere

Published: 1887

Meaning: "Touch Me Not" (Latin phrase from the Bible)

Setting: A fictional town representing colonial Philippines

Main Themes:

  • Corruption of Spanish friars
  • Abuse of colonial authorities
  • Social inequality and injustice
  • The need for education and reform

Impact: Immediately banned in the Philippines, the novel opened Filipino eyes to colonial abuses and sparked nationalist sentiment.

Main Character: Crisostomo Ibarra, a young Filipino who returns from Europe to find his country oppressed.

El Filibusterismo

Published: 1891

Meaning: "The Reign of Greed" or "The Filibustering"

Setting: Continuation of Noli Me Tangere, set 13 years later

Main Themes:

  • Revolution vs. reform
  • Consequences of oppression
  • Corruption in government and church
  • The futility of violence

Impact: Showed the darker consequences of colonial rule and influenced revolutionary leaders like Andr√©s Bonifacio.

Main Character: Simoun (Ibarra's alter ego), a wealthy jeweler plotting revolution against the Spanish government.

Historical Significance

These novels were revolutionary not just in content but in approach. Rizal used literature as a weapon against oppression, proving that the pen could indeed be mightier than the sword. They:

  • Exposed the reality of colonial life to both Filipinos and Europeans
  • Inspired the formation of the Katipunan revolutionary society
  • Became required reading under the Rizal Law (RA 1425)
  • Continue to influence Filipino literature and social consciousness today
